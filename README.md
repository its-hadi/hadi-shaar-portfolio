# Holistic Signature Work Reflection

A major theme in my portfolio is using technology to create access to meaningful experiences. Through my work in computer science, virtual reality, web development, business, and education, I have learned that technology should not only be judged by whether it works technically. It should also be judged by whether it helps people understand, participate, learn, and connect. This theme matters to me because many of my strongest experiences at the University of St. Thomas have involved building tools that are meant for real users, not just completing assignments. Whether I was working on a capstone project, a virtual reality application, a website, or an interactive tour, I had to think about people on the other side of the screen.

This connects to the common good because technology has the power to either include people or leave them out. A system can be technically impressive but still confusing, inaccessible, or disconnected from the needs of the people using it. My St. Thomas education has helped me understand that computer science is not separate from ethics, communication, business, education, and community impact. I see my work as a combination of technical problem solving and human-centered thinking. The goal is not just to build something, but to build something that is useful, clear, responsible, and connected to a real purpose.

My capstone project, GreenStepEmployee, is one example of this growth. The project focused on sustainability, employee participation, and organizational impact. From a computer science perspective, the project required planning, design, development, database thinking, and user interface work. However, the project was not only about code. It was also about how software can support better habits, encourage participation, and help organizations connect individual actions to larger goals. This helped me see sustainability as both a technical and social problem. The technical side involved building a system that functioned correctly. The social side involved thinking about how people would understand it, use it, and see value in it.

My learning across disciplines shaped the way I understood that project. Computer science gave me the tools to build and organize the application. Business and marketing helped me think about how users respond to design, messaging, and motivation. Ethical thinking helped me ask whether the technology was serving a meaningful purpose. Those different lenses changed how I approached the work. Instead of thinking only about features, I started thinking about impact. I learned that software projects are stronger when they are connected to real human behavior and clear communication.

My work at the University of St. Thomas VR Lab also shaped my understanding of technology and access. As a VR Development Intern, I worked with tools such as 3DVista, Unity, C#, and other VR technologies. I helped develop interactive VR applications, manage projects, train users, and support VR film production. This experience showed me that advanced technology is only valuable if people can actually use it. Virtual reality can be exciting, but it can also be intimidating for users who are unfamiliar with the tools. I had to learn how to explain technical steps clearly, test interactions, and make the experience easier for others to understand.

This experience fits the SOAR pattern. The situation was that the VR Lab needed projects and training that were both technically functional and approachable for users. The obstacle was that VR tools can be complex, and people may feel uncomfortable when they do not know how to use the equipment or software. My action was to help develop VR content, train users, test interactions, and improve the clarity of the experience. The result was that I grew not only in technical VR development, but also in communication, teaching, patience, and user-centered design. This became one of the clearest examples of how my technical and professional skills complemented each other.

The Coffee Shop VR Application is another example of interdisciplinary learning. This project connected computer science with Spanish, education, and user experience design. The goal was to support conversational Spanish learning through an immersive environment. Technically, it involved VR tools, 360-degree media, and editing. But the larger purpose was educational. The project made me think about how people learn and how environment affects confidence. A language learner may feel nervous practicing in a real situation, so a virtual coffee shop can provide a lower-pressure space to practice. This helped me understand that technology can support learning when it is designed around the user’s experience.

The University of St. Thomas Virtual Tour also reflects this theme of access. A virtual campus tour can help people explore a place before they physically visit it. This matters because not everyone has the same ability, time, or opportunity to visit in person. By using interactive hotspots, multimedia, and accessible navigation, the project connected technical design with communication and inclusion. It taught me that accessibility is not only about meeting a requirement. It is about respecting the user and making information easier to reach.

One of my strongest failure-learning-change experiences came from my Software Engineer Internship at Taxfully. At first, when I worked on website updates, I focused mostly on making the changes look right visually. I wanted the page to display correctly and match what was expected. However, I learned that a website is more than what appears on the surface. Small changes can affect page load time, search visibility, user experience, and the business purpose of the site. If a page looks fine but loads slowly, confuses users, or is not structured well for SEO, then the work is incomplete.

What did not go as well as I hoped was my early narrow focus. I was thinking like someone trying to finish a task, not fully like someone responsible for the user and business impact. What I learned was that technical work needs context. I needed to think about performance, content structure, analytics, and why the update mattered. What I changed was my approach. I began paying more attention to speed, clarity, SEO, and how web development connects with marketing. This experience helped me grow because it showed me that being a good developer means understanding both the code and the purpose behind the code.

My Star Map Application also helped me grow technically. The project involved a 3D star map using Swift, Python, and Unity3D with real-time sky exploration through gyroscope and touch navigation. This required debugging, performance improvement, accurate positioning, and smoother interaction. It strengthened my problem-solving skills because user interaction has to feel natural. Even if the logic is correct, the experience can still feel frustrating if the app is slow or difficult to control. This project helped me understand that technical achievement and user experience are connected.

Across these experiences, my perspective on technology’s role in society has changed. Earlier, I often thought of technology mainly as a set of tools, languages, and systems. Now I see it more as a way of shaping experiences. Technology affects how people learn, communicate, access information, participate in organizations, and understand the world around them. That makes technical work a responsibility. Developers make choices that influence users, even in small ways.

My St. Thomas education prepared me for this by pushing me to connect technical learning with broader questions. Computer science gave me a foundation in programming, databases, algorithms, and development. My business and marketing background helped me understand users, communication, and practical value. My VR and educational projects helped me see how technology can support learning and access. Together, these experiences helped me build a professional identity based on both competence and care.

After graduation, I plan to continue developing both skill sets. Technically, I want to keep improving in software development, databases, web applications, and interactive technologies. Professionally, I want to keep improving my communication, teamwork, leadership, and ability to explain complex ideas clearly. The experiences in this portfolio show that my growth has not only been about learning how to build things. It has been about learning how to build things for people. That is the kind of work I want to continue doing.
